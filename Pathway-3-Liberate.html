Choosing Your Business Model

There are four main business models for service-based businesses. Most successful business owners eventually use a combination, but you need to start with one.

MODEL 1: DONE-FOR-YOU SERVICES

What it is: You do the actual work for clients. They tell you what they need, you deliver it.

Examples:

  • Virtual assistant services (email management, calendar management, travel booking)
  • Bookkeeping and financial reporting
  • Social media management
  • Content writing
  • Event coordination
  • Data entry and database management

Pros:

  • Easiest to start (you already know how to do this work)
  • Clients understand what they're buying
  • Predictable scope and deliverables
  • Can start immediately with existing skills

Cons:

  • You're trading time for money (limited scalability)
  • Can become repetitive
  • Income stops if you stop working
  • May be undervalued if you price per hour

Best for: Women who want to start fast, have clear existing skills, and need income quickly.

MODEL 2: CONSULTING & ADVISORY

What it is: You analyze situations, provide recommendations, and create strategies. The client implements.

Examples:

  • Process improvement consulting
  • Business systems audits
  • Operational efficiency reviews
  • Compliance and policy development
  • Strategic planning facilitation

Pros:

  • Higher fees (you're paid for expertise, not execution)
  • More interesting work (different problems, different clients)
  • Defined project scopes
  • Builds your reputation as an expert

Cons:

  • Requires more confidence to position yourself as expert
  • Harder to get first clients (they need to trust your judgment)
  • Need proven track record or case studies
  • Selling is harder (less tangible deliverable)

Best for: Women with 10+ years experience in a specific area, strong analytical skills, and ability to identify patterns.

MODEL 3: TRAINING & EDUCATION

What it is: You teach others how to do what you know. Workshops, courses, group training.

Examples:

  • Software training (Excel, project management tools, CRM systems)
  • Professional skills workshops (communication, time management, organization)
  • Industry-specific training (compliance, procedures, regulations)
  • Online courses
  • Group coaching programs

Pros:

  • One-to-many model (teach multiple people at once)
  • Course content can be reused/repurposed
  • Can be partially passive (pre-recorded content)
  • Builds authority and visibility

Cons:

  • Takes time to develop quality training materials
  • Need to be comfortable presenting/teaching
  • Marketing can be challenging
  • May need initial investment in tools/platforms

Best for: Women who enjoy teaching, have patience for explaining concepts, and want to leverage their time.

MODEL 4: HYBRID (IMPLEMENTATION + CONSULTING)

What it is: You design the solution AND implement it. Strategy plus execution.

Examples:

  • Process redesign AND training staff on new process
  • System selection, implementation, and staff training
  • Policy development AND rollout/communication
  • Website content strategy AND writing the actual content

Pros:

  • Higher total project value
  • Clients get complete solution (easier to sell)
  • You control the outcome
  • Can command premium rates

Cons:

  • Longer project timelines
  • More complex scoping and pricing
  • Need diverse skill set
  • Can become time-intensive

Best for: Women who like seeing complete results, have both strategic and execution skills, and can manage complex projects.

Chapter 3: Business Structure & Legal Basics

Legal Stuff, Demystified (For People Who Aren't Lawyers)

This chapter will not make you a legal expert. It will give you enough knowledge to make informed decisions and know when to get professional advice.

The legal side of business intimidates most people. That's intentional — lawyers benefit from complexity. But the basics of business structure, registration, and protection aren't actually that complicated once you strip away the jargon.

IMPORTANT: This chapter provides general information about Australian business structures and requirements. Laws vary by state and change over time. Always verify current requirements and consult professionals for your specific situation.

I am not a lawyer, accountant, or financial advisor. I'm someone who's started multiple businesses and learned these things the hard way so you don't have to.

Business Structures: What They Are and Which One You Need

Your business structure affects your taxes, liability, paperwork, and costs. There are four main options in Australia. Here's what you actually need to know about each:

SOLE TRADER

What it is: You and your business are legally the same entity. Simplest structure possible.

Setup requirements:

  • Register for an ABN (Australian Business Number) — free, online, takes 15 minutes
  • Register a business name if you want to trade under a name other than your own (optional, $37 for 1 year or $87 for 3 years)
  • That's it. You're in business.

Taxes:

  • Business income is your personal income
  • Report on your personal tax return
  • Pay tax at your personal income tax rate
  • No separate business tax return required
  • Can claim business expenses against income

Liability:

  • You are personally liable for all business debts
  • If the business is sued, your personal assets are at risk
  • No separation between business and personal finances legally (though you should separate them practically)

Pros:

  • Cheapest to set up (basically free)
  • Easiest to manage (minimal paperwork)
  • Simple tax reporting
  • Full control over all decisions
  • Can start immediately

Cons:

  • Personal liability for business debts
  • Harder to raise capital
  • Perceived as less professional by some clients
  • Business ends if you die or become incapacitated

Best for: Service-based businesses with low financial risk, solopreneurs, anyone just starting out and testing their business idea.

PARTNERSHIP

What it is: Two or more people in business together, sharing profits and liability.

Setup requirements:

  • Register for an ABN
  • Register business name (required if not using partners' names)
  • Create partnership agreement (highly recommended, though not legally required)

Taxes:

  • Partnership lodges tax return but doesn't pay tax
  • Each partner reports their share of income on personal tax return
  • Each partner pays tax at their personal rate

Liability:

  • Each partner is personally liable for all partnership debts
  • You can be held responsible for your partner's business decisions
  • Personal assets at risk

Pros:

  • Relatively simple to set up
  • Shared workload and expertise
  • Shared financial burden
  • Can be more affordable than starting alone

Cons:

  • Shared liability (you're responsible for partner's mistakes)
  • Shared profits
  • Potential for conflict
  • Requires high level of trust
  • More complex than sole trader

Best for: Two people with complementary skills who trust each other completely and have compatible work ethics and values.

WARNING ABOUT PARTNERSHIPS: I've seen more partnerships end badly than succeed. If you're considering a partnership, get a written agreement BEFORE you start. Cover: profit split, decision-making authority, what happens if someone wants out, dispute resolution. And know that even the best friendships can be destroyed by business partnerships gone wrong.

COMPANY (PTY LTD)

What it is: A separate legal entity. The company exists independently from you.

Setup requirements:

  • Register with ASIC (Australian Securities and Investments Commission)
  • Costs $538 for registration (as of 2024)
  • Annual ASIC fee: $289
  • Must have at least one director and one shareholder (can be the same person)
  • More complex ongoing compliance requirements

Taxes:

  • Company pays tax on its profits at company tax rate (25% for small businesses with turnover under $50M, 30% otherwise)
  • You pay yourself a salary (taxed as personal income)
  • Dividends to shareholders may have tax implications
  • Separate tax return for the company
  • More complex accounting required

Liability:

  • Limited liability — your personal assets are generally protected
  • The company is liable for its debts, not you personally (with some exceptions)
  • Directors can be held personally liable in certain circumstances (fraud, trading while insolvent)

Pros:

  • Limited personal liability
  • Perceived as more professional
  • Easier to raise capital
  • Can have multiple shareholders/investors
  • Continues even if director/shareholder changes
  • Potentially better tax structure as business grows

Cons:

  • Higher setup costs
  • Ongoing ASIC fees
  • More complex accounting and tax requirements
  • More regulatory compliance
  • Annual financial statements required
  • More expensive to run (accountant fees higher)

Best for: Businesses with higher financial risk, those planning to grow significantly, businesses seeking investment, or when asset protection is important.

TRUST

What it is: A structure where a trustee holds assets and operates the business on behalf of beneficiaries.

I'm not covering trusts in detail because:

  • They're complex and expensive to set up
  • They're rarely the right choice for a starting service business
  • If you need a trust, you need a lawyer and accountant to advise you

Trusts are sometimes used for asset protection or tax planning in established businesses, but they're overkill for most people reading this guide.

Which Structure Should You Choose?

MY RECOMMENDATION FOR MOST WOMEN STARTING OUT:

Start as a sole trader.

Why? Because you need to get to revenue fast, not spend months and thousands of dollars setting up complex structures for a business that doesn't exist yet.

Start simple. Prove the business works. Make money. Then upgrade your structure if needed.

Start as sole trader if:

  • You're testing a business idea
  • You're providing low-risk services (consulting, virtual assistance, training)
  • You need to minimize startup costs
  • You want to get started immediately
  • Your projected revenue in year one is under $75,000

Consider a company if:

  • You're in a high-risk industry
  • You're handling other people's money
  • You're manufacturing products
  • You expect revenue over $100,000 in year one
  • You want to bring on investors or partners
  • Your accountant specifically recommends it for tax purposes

The truth: You can always change structures later. Many successful businesses start as sole traders and incorporate once they're profitable and growing. Don't let analysis paralysis stop you from starting.

Registration Requirements: What You Actually Need

ABN (AUSTRALIAN BUSINESS NUMBER)

What it is: A unique 11-digit number that identifies your business.

Do you need one? Yes, if you're running a business. No, if you're just earning casual income.

How to get one:

  • Go to abr.gov.au
  • Click "Apply for an ABN"
  • Answer the questions honestly
  • It's free
  • Takes about 15 minutes
  • Usually approved immediately (sometimes takes a few days)

What you need to apply:

  • Your personal details (name, date of birth, address)
  • Your Tax File Number (TFN)
  • Description of your business activities
  • Expected start date

BUSINESS NAME REGISTRATION

What it is: The name you trade under if it's not your personal name.

Do you need one?

  • NO if you're trading as "Jane Smith Consulting" and your name is Jane Smith
  • YES if you're trading as "Smith Solutions" or any name that's not your legal name

How to register:

  • Through ASIC (asic.gov.au)
  • Costs: $37 for 1 year, $87 for 3 years
  • Search first to ensure name isn't already taken
  • Must renew before expiry or lose the name

Tips for choosing a business name:

  • Check domain name availability first (.com.au or .au)
  • Make it easy to spell and remember
  • Avoid names that limit future growth (e.g., "Perth Social Media" if you might expand to other cities/services)
  • Search ASIC database to ensure it's available
  • Don't spend weeks agonizing over this — you can change it later

GST REGISTRATION

What it is: Goods and Services Tax — a 10% tax on most goods and services in Australia.

Do you need to register?

  • REQUIRED if your annual turnover is $75,000 or more
  • OPTIONAL if your turnover is under $75,000
  • You can voluntarily register even under $75,000

Should you register if you're under $75,000?

Maybe. Here's when to consider it:

  • Register if: You have significant business expenses you can claim GST credits on (equipment, software, contractors)
  • Register if: Your clients are businesses (they can claim the GST back, so it doesn't affect your competitiveness)
  • Register if: You expect to exceed $75,000 within the year
  • Don't register if: Your clients are consumers (individuals) and you have minimal business expenses — adding 10% GST to your prices makes you less competitive

What it means if you register:

  • You charge 10% GST on your services
  • You can claim GST credits on business expenses
  • You lodge BAS (Business Activity Statement) quarterly or annually
  • More accounting and bookkeeping required

MY RECOMMENDATION: If you're starting out under $75,000 turnover and serving individual clients (not businesses), don't register for GST initially. Wait until you're required to or until your accountant advises it makes sense for your situation.

One less thing to manage while you're getting started.

Professional licenses or certifications:

Insurance: What You Actually Need

Insurance is boring and feels like wasted money until you need it. Here's what to consider:

PUBLIC LIABILITY INSURANCE

What it covers: Claims if you accidentally injure someone or damage their property while conducting business.

Do you need it?

  • YES if you visit client premises
  • YES if clients visit your premises
  • YES if clients require it (many do)
  • PROBABLY if you work in person with anyone
  • MAYBE NOT if you work entirely remotely and never meet clients

Cost: Approximately $300-800 per year for basic coverage ($10M-$20M cover)

Example scenario: You're at a client's office for a meeting. You trip and spill hot coffee on their laptop, destroying it and causing $3,000 in damage. Public liability insurance covers this.

PROFESSIONAL INDEMNITY INSURANCE

What it covers: Claims arising from professional advice, errors, or negligence in your work.

Do you need it?

  • YES if you provide advice or consulting
  • YES if errors in your work could cause financial loss to clients
  • YES if contracts require it
  • REQUIRED for some professions (financial advisors, accountants, lawyers)
  • RECOMMENDED for most professional services

Cost: Approximately $500-2,000+ per year depending on profession and coverage amount

Example scenario: You provide business advice that the client follows, but it results in financial loss. They sue you for negligence. Professional indemnity insurance covers your legal defense and any settlement.

INCOME PROTECTION INSURANCE

What it covers: Replaces portion of your income if you can't work due to illness or injury.

Do you need it?

  • HIGHLY RECOMMENDED if you're the sole income earner
  • CONSIDER if you have dependents
  • CONSIDER if you have no safety net
  • LESS URGENT if you have a working partner or significant savings

Cost: Varies significantly based on age, health, occupation, waiting period, and benefit period. Approximately $50-200+ per month.

Reality check: As a self-employed person, if you can't work, you don't get paid. No sick leave. No income. This insurance can be the difference between financial survival and disaster.

CYBER LIABILITY INSURANCE

What it covers: Data breaches, cyber attacks, ransomware, loss of client data.

Do you need it?

  • CONSIDER if you handle client data
  • CONSIDER if you store sensitive information
  • PROBABLY NOT if you're just starting out with minimal data handling

Cost: Approximately $500-1,500+ per year

MY INSURANCE RECOMMENDATIONS FOR STARTING OUT:

MINIMUM: Public liability insurance ($10M cover) — $300-500/year

STRONGLY RECOMMENDED: Professional indemnity insurance if providing advice/consulting — $500-1,500/year

PERSONAL PRIORITY: Income protection insurance if you have no safety net — $50-150/month

Total annual cost for basic protection: $1,500-3,000

Yes, it's expensive. Yes, it's necessary. Budget for it from the start.

Contracts and Agreements: Protecting Yourself

You need contracts. Not because you distrust clients. Because clarity prevents conflicts and protects both parties.

WHY YOU NEED WRITTEN CONTRACTS

I've learned this the hard way: verbal agreements aren't worth the paper they're not written on.

Written contracts protect you from:

  • "I thought it included..." (scope creep)
  • "I didn't know it would cost that much" (payment disputes)
  • "You didn't deliver what we agreed" (unclear deliverables)
  • "I'll pay you next month" (delayed payment)
  • "We never agreed to that timeline" (expectation mismatches)

WHAT EVERY SERVICE CONTRACT SHOULD INCLUDE

1. Parties: Who is providing services (you/your business) and who is receiving them (client)

2. Scope of work: Exactly what you're delivering. Be specific:

  • What IS included
  • What is NOT included
  • Deliverables (what client will receive)
  • Timeline (when things will be delivered)

3. Payment terms:

  • Total cost
  • Payment structure (upfront, milestone-based, upon completion)
  • When payment is due
  • Late payment penalties
  • What happens if client doesn't pay

4. Timelines and deadlines:

  • Project start date
  • Milestone dates
  • Final delivery date
  • What happens if deadlines are missed (by either party)

5. Revision/change policy:

  • How many rounds of revisions included
  • Cost for additional revisions
  • Process for requesting changes
  • How scope changes are handled

6. Termination clause:

  • How either party can end the agreement
  • Notice period required
  • What happens to payment if contract is terminated early
  • What happens to work in progress

7. Confidentiality:

  • Agreement not to share client's confidential information
  • What constitutes confidential information
  • Exceptions (publicly available information, legally required disclosure)

8. Intellectual property:

  • Who owns the work product
  • When ownership transfers (usually upon full payment)
  • Your right to use work in portfolio (with client permission)

9. Liability limitations:

  • What you're NOT responsible for
  • Limits on your liability
  • Disclaimer of warranties

10. Signatures and dates:

  • Both parties must sign
  • Date of agreement
  • Electronic signatures are legally valid

DO YOU NEED A LAWYER?

For starting out: Probably not. You can use contract templates and modify them for your needs.

When to get a lawyer:

  • High-value contracts (over $20,000)
  • Complex projects with significant risk
  • When client insists on their contract and you don't understand it
  • If you're entering an industry with specific legal requirements
  • Once you can afford it, get a lawyer to review/create your standard contract template

Cost for lawyer to draft/review contract: $500-2,000 depending on complexity

Chapter 4: Pricing & Money

The Hardest Part: Charging What You're Worth

This is where decades of systematic undervaluation will fight you the hardest.

You've been conditioned to accept less. To be grateful for whatever you're offered. To never ask for too much. To prove you're worth it before you're paid fairly. To feel guilty about money.

Now you have to set your own rates. And that voice in your head — the one that's been trained by every underpaying employer you've ever had — will tell you that you're asking for too much. That nobody will pay that. That you're not worth it.

That voice is a liar.

TRUTH: The rate that feels slightly uncomfortable is probably the right rate. If you feel completely comfortable with your pricing, you're probably undercharging.

Your discomfort is not evidence that you're overpricing. It's evidence of internalized undervaluation fighting against fair compensation.

Why Hourly Pricing Is a Trap (Especially for Women)

Most women starting a service business default to hourly pricing. It feels safer. More justifiable. Easier to defend.

It's also the fastest way to recreate the exact undervaluation problem you're trying to escape.

Here's why hourly pricing hurts you:

  • It punishes efficiency: The better you get at your work, the faster you complete it, the less you earn. You're literally penalized for being good at what you do.
  • It limits your income: You can only work so many hours. Your income has a hard ceiling.
  • It undervalues expertise: You're charging for time, not results. A task that takes you 2 hours because of 20 years of experience is worth more than a task that takes someone else 8 hours because they're learning.
  • It invites micromanagement: Clients who pay hourly often track your time obsessively, questioning every minute.
  • It focuses on cost, not value: You're framing your service as an expense (hours of time) rather than an investment (problem solved, result achieved).

The only person hourly pricing benefits is the client. And even then, not really — because you'll either burn out or limit the quality of work you can provide.

BUT WHAT IF CLIENTS ASK FOR YOUR HOURLY RATE?

They will. Here's what to say:

"I don't price by the hour because I've found that doesn't serve either of us well. Instead, I price based on the value of the outcome. For [specific deliverable/result], my fee is [amount]. This gives you certainty on cost and ensures I'm focused on results, not clock-watching."

If they push back:

"If you need an hourly breakdown for budgeting purposes, I can tell you this project typically requires approximately [X] hours of work, which at industry standard rates would be $[amount]. My project fee of $[amount] reflects both the work required and the value of the outcome. Most clients find this more straightforward than tracking hours."

Some clients will insist on hourly. That's fine. Just make sure your hourly rate is high enough that you're not recreating employment-level pay.

Value-Based Pricing: What It Is and How It Works

Value-based pricing means: You charge based on the value the client receives, not the time it takes you to deliver it.

Example:

You help a business streamline their invoicing process. It takes you 8 hours. The new process saves them 10 hours per week in admin time.

  • Hourly pricing: 8 hours × $50/hour = $400
  • Value-based pricing: 10 hours saved per week × 50 weeks = 500 hours saved per year × $30/hour (their admin cost) = $15,000 annual value. You charge $2,000-3,000 for the project.

Same work. Different framing. Different price. Fair compensation for the value you created.

How to price based on value:

  • Identify the problem's cost: What is this problem costing them in time, money, opportunity, stress?
  • Quantify the solution's value: How much will they save/earn/gain by solving this problem?
  • Price as percentage of value: Typically 10-30% of first-year value, depending on complexity and your expertise

IMPORTANT: Value-based pricing only works when you can articulate and quantify the value. If you can't explain why solving this problem is worth $5,000, you can't charge $5,000.

This is why discovery conversations are critical. You need to understand the actual business impact of the problem you're solving.

Project-Based Pricing: The Practical Middle Ground

For most service businesses starting out, project-based pricing is the sweet spot.

What it is: You charge a fixed fee for a defined scope of work with clear deliverables.

Example structures:

  • "$2,500 for complete bookkeeping setup including chart of accounts, integration with your systems, and 2 hours of training"
  • "$1,800 for quarterly financial reports package including P&L, balance sheet, cash flow analysis, and 1-hour results review meeting"
  • "$3,500 for process documentation project covering 5 key workflows with diagrams, written procedures, and staff training session"

Why this works:

  • Client knows exactly what they're paying
  • You're not penalized for efficiency
  • Scope is clear (reduces scope creep)
  • You can increase rates based on value, not just time
  • Easier to compare to hourly alternatives (clients can see the value)

If this work creates significant value (saves money, generates revenue, solves urgent problem), add 20-50% premium

$2,600 becomes $2,500 or $2,750 (ending in 00, 50, 75, or 99)

The Psychology of Pricing: Overcoming Your Internalized Undervaluation

The math might tell you to charge $150/hour. The market research might confirm that's reasonable. But that voice in your head will still say, "Nobody will pay that. You're not worth that. That's too much."

Let's address that voice directly.

COMMON PRICING FEARS (AND THE TRUTH)

Fear: "Nobody will pay that much."

Truth: Some people won't. Others will. You're not trying to serve everyone. You're trying to serve clients who value quality work and can afford to pay for it. Those clients exist, and they're actively looking for someone reliable.

Fear: "I don't have enough experience to charge that."

Truth: You have decades of experience. You just don't have decades of experience being paid fairly for it. That's different. Your skills are valuable regardless of whether you've been compensated fairly for them in the past.

Fear: "What if they say no?"

Truth: Some will. That's fine. "No" is information. It either means they can't afford you (not your customer), they don't see the value (wrong fit), or your positioning needs work (fixable). Every "no" brings you closer to the right "yes."

Fear: "I feel guilty charging that much."

Truth: You feel guilty because you've been trained to accept less. That guilt is not evidence that you're overcharging. It's evidence of systematic conditioning. Men don't feel guilty about charging market rates. Neither should you.

Fear: "What if I'm not as good as they expect for that price?"

Truth: Imposter syndrome is not evidence of incompetence. If you can deliver the results you're promising, you're worth the price. The fact that you worry about this at all suggests you're probably more conscientious than most people charging the same rates.

THE UNCOMFORTABLE TRUTH ABOUT PRICING:

You will always feel like you're charging too much until you normalize receiving fair compensation.

That normalization doesn't happen by thinking about it. It happens by charging appropriately and seeing that clients pay it, receive value, and come back for more.

You have to do the uncomfortable thing before you feel comfortable doing it.

Handling Price Objections

Clients will question your pricing. Here's how to respond with confidence:

"THAT'S TOO EXPENSIVE"

What NOT to say: "I can lower it" or "Sorry, let me see what I can do"

What TO say:

"I understand this is an investment. Let me make sure you understand what you're getting for that investment: [restate value, outcomes, what's included]. When you consider [specific valuable outcome], most clients find this represents excellent value. Does that make sense?"

Or:

"I appreciate that price is a consideration. Can I ask what you were expecting to invest in solving this problem? That will help me understand if we're on the same page about the scope and value."

"I CAN GET IT CHEAPER ELSEWHERE"

What NOT to say: "I'll match their price"

What TO say:

"You absolutely can find cheaper options, and you should explore those if price is your primary consideration. The difference with my service is [specific differentiator: expertise, reliability, outcome guarantee, etc.]. My clients choose to work with me because they value [quality/results/expertise] over lowest price. If that aligns with your priorities, I'd love to work with you. If lowest price is what matters most, I'm probably not the right fit."

"CAN YOU DO IT FOR [LOWER AMOUNT]?"

What NOT to say: "Okay" (unless you genuinely can)

What TO say:

"I price my services based on the value delivered and the expertise required. I'm not able to lower the price for the full scope we discussed. However, if budget is a constraint, we could look at a reduced scope that fits your budget. What if we started with [smaller deliverable] for [lower price]? That would give you [partial outcome] and we could expand from there if it makes sense."

"I NEED TO THINK ABOUT IT"

What NOT to say: "Okay, let me know" (and then never hear from them again)

What TO say:

"Absolutely, this is an important decision. Can I ask what specific concerns you need to think through? [Listen] I'm happy to address those now if that would be helpful. If you need time, that's fine — when should I follow up with you?"

Then actually follow up when you said you would.

When to Discount (and When Not To)

Do NOT discount because:

  • You're nervous about your pricing
  • The client asked
  • You think they might not pay full price
  • You want to be liked
  • You feel guilty

DO discount strategically when:

  • You're doing pilot projects to test your offer (2-3 clients maximum)
  • Client will provide substantial testimonial/case study/referrals (get this in writing)
  • It's a long-term retainer commitment (12+ months)
  • Client is genuinely budget-constrained but perfect fit for case study or market entry
  • It's a nonprofit or cause you genuinely support (and you can afford it)

If you do discount:

  • Always show the full price and the discounted price
  • Explain why you're discounting (limited pilot spots, case study participation, etc.)
  • Set clear expectations about future pricing
  • Get something in return (testimonial, referrals, case study rights)

WARNING: Every time you discount unnecessarily, you reinforce the idea that your work isn't worth your stated price. You train clients to expect discounts. You devalue your own expertise.

The client who pushes for a discount rarely becomes your best client. They're often the most demanding and least satisfied, because they fundamentally don't value what you do.

Chapter 6: Selling Your Services

Why Most Women Hate Selling (And How to Fix It)

Let's address the elephant in the room: you probably hate the idea of selling.

Not because you're bad at it. Because you've been conditioned to believe that selling is manipulative, pushy, aggressive, sleazy — all the things women are punished for being.

Add to that decades of being underpaid, and you've got a recipe for massive discomfort: you already struggle to believe you're worth what you charge, and now you have to convince someone else to pay you?

Here's the reframe that changed everything for me:

Selling is not convincing someone to buy something they don't need.

Selling is helping someone solve a problem they already have by clearly explaining how you can help them.

You're not manipulating. You're clarifying. You're not pushing. You're offering. You're not taking advantage. You're providing a solution.

The problem exists whether you sell to them or not. The question is: will you help them solve it, or will they continue struggling (or hire someone less qualified)?

The Discovery Call: Where Sales Actually Happens

Most people think the sale happens when you send a proposal or quote. It doesn't. The sale happens during the discovery call — the conversation where you understand their problem and they understand how you can solve it.

Get this right, and the proposal is just a formality. Get this wrong, and no amount of follow-up will save it.

Duration: 30-45 minutes

Goal: Determine if there's a good fit, understand their problem deeply, and help them see you as the solution.

Structure:

  • 5 minutes: Rapport building and setting expectations
  • 20-25 minutes: Discovery questions (you listen, they talk)
  • 5-10 minutes: Explain how you can help
  • 5 minutes: Next steps and closing

"Thanks so much for making time to chat. I'm looking forward to learning more about [Company/their situation]."


"Here's what I thought we could do with our time today: I'd love to hear about [the challenge they mentioned/their current situation], understand what you're trying to achieve, and see if there's a way I can help. Then I can share what that might look like working together. Does that sound good?"


"Before we dive in, tell me a bit about your role and what you're responsible for at [Company]."

Why this works:

  • Sets clear expectations for the call
  • Establishes you as organized and professional
  • Gets them talking about themselves (people like this)
  • Positions you as consultant, not desperate salesperson

DISCOVERY QUESTIONS (NEXT 20-25 MINUTES)

This is the most important part of the call. Your job is to ask questions and LISTEN. The more they talk, the better.

The question framework:

1. Understand the current situation

"Tell me about how you're handling [specific area of your service] right now."


"Walk me through your current process for [relevant task/challenge]."


"Who's responsible for [relevant area] currently?"

2. Identify the pain/problem

"What's working well with your current approach?"


"What's not working as well as you'd like?"


"What prompted you to start looking for help with this now?"


"If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about [situation], what would it be?"

3. Quantify the impact

"How much time does your team spend on [problem] each week?"


"What's this costing you in terms of time/money/opportunity?"


"What happens if this problem doesn't get solved in the next 3-6 months?"


"What would be different if this was working smoothly?"

4. Understand decision-making

"Who else is involved in making this decision?"


"What's your timeline for addressing this?"


"Have you looked at other solutions or providers?"


"What would make this a successful engagement for you?"

5. Uncover budget/constraints

"Have you allocated budget for solving this?"


"What range were you thinking for an investment like this?"


"Are there any constraints I should be aware of — timing, budget, resources?"

CRITICAL DISCOVERY SKILL: SHUT UP AND LISTEN

Your instinct will be to jump in with solutions the moment you hear a problem. Resist this.

Let them talk. Ask follow-up questions. Go deeper. The more you understand, the better solution you can propose — and the more convinced they'll be that you understand their situation.

Rule: They should talk 70% of the time. You ask questions and listen 70% of the time. You explain your solution 30% of the time.

PRESENTING YOUR SOLUTION (NEXT 5-10 MINUTES)

Now that you understand their situation, explain how you can help. But don't just describe your service — connect it directly to what they told you.

"Based on what you've shared, here's how I'd approach this:"


"You mentioned [specific problem they described]. What I'd do is [specific solution], which would give you [specific outcome they want]."


"You also mentioned [second problem]. To address that, I'd [specific approach], which should [desired result]."


"The outcome you'd see is [summarize benefits in their language, using their words where possible]."


"Does this sound like what you're looking for?"

Why this works:

  • You're using their words and priorities (shows you listened)
  • You're connecting your service to their specific situation (not generic pitch)
  • You're focused on outcomes they care about (not features of your service)
  • You're checking for alignment before moving to price

ADDRESSING CONCERNS (AS THEY COME UP)

They might raise objections or concerns during the call. Address them immediately:

"How long will this take?"

"Based on what you've described, I'd estimate [timeframe]. The timeline depends on [factors], but I'd give you a detailed project plan showing milestones so you know exactly what to expect and when."

"We tried something like this before and it didn't work."

"Tell me more about what you tried and what didn't work. [Listen] I can see why that was frustrating. The approach I'd take is different in that [explain difference]. Would that address the issues you had before?"

"I'm not sure we have the budget for this right now."

"I understand budget is always a consideration. Can I ask what you were thinking in terms of investment range? That will help me think through what might be possible. [If they give range] Okay, let me think about how we could structure this to fit that budget while still solving the key problems you mentioned."

CLOSING THE CALL (FINAL 5 MINUTES)

Don't leave the call without clear next steps.

If they're ready to move forward:

"This sounds like a great fit. Here's what I'd suggest for next steps: I'll send you a detailed proposal by [specific date] outlining exactly what we discussed — scope, timeline, investment. You can review it, and we can schedule a quick call to discuss any questions. Once you're happy with everything, I'll send over the contract and we can get started. How does that sound?"

If they need to think about it or discuss with others:

"I understand this is an important decision. Can I ask what specific concerns or questions you need to work through? [Listen] Okay, that makes sense. Would it be helpful if I sent you a proposal outlining everything we discussed? That way you have something concrete to review and discuss with [stakeholders]. When would be a good time to follow up with you?"

If it's not a fit:

"Based on our conversation, I'm not sure I'm the right fit for what you need. [Explain why honestly] I'd recommend [alternative suggestion or referral if you have one]. But if circumstances change or you need help with [related service you do offer], I'd be happy to chat again."

ALWAYS END WITH A SPECIFIC NEXT ACTION:

▸ "I'll send the proposal by Friday"
▸ "Let's schedule a follow-up call for next Tuesday"
▸ "I'll check with my network and send you some referrals by tomorrow"

Never end with "Let me know if you're interested." That puts the burden on them and you'll probably never hear back.

Handling Objections Like a Pro

Objections aren't rejections. They're requests for more information or reassurance. Here's how to handle the most common ones:

"YOUR PRICE IS TOO HIGH"

What's really being said: "I don't see the value yet" OR "I can't afford this" OR "I need to negotiate because that's what I always do"

How to respond:

"I understand price is an important consideration. Can I ask what you were expecting to invest in solving this problem? [Listen]


The reason for my pricing is that this work will [specific value/outcome]. When you consider that you're currently [spending/losing/wasting] [amount] on this problem, this investment pays for itself in [timeframe].


That said, if budget is a constraint, we could look at a reduced scope. What if we started with just [Phase 1] for [lower price]? That would address [primary problem] and we could expand from there if it makes sense."

"I NEED TO THINK ABOUT IT"

What's really being said: "I have a concern I haven't voiced" OR "I need to discuss with someone" OR "I'm avoiding making a decision"

How to respond:

"Absolutely, I completely understand. Can I ask what specific aspects you want to think through? Is it the pricing, the timeline, the scope, or something else? [Listen]


[Address the specific concern]


Is there anything I can clarify that would help you make this decision? And what's your timeline for making a decision? I'm happy to schedule a follow-up call for [specific date] to discuss any questions that come up."

"WE'RE TALKING TO OTHER PROVIDERS"

What's really being said: "Convince me you're the best choice" OR "I want you to know you're not the only option"

How to respond:

"That's smart — you should talk to multiple providers to find the right fit. Can I ask what criteria you're using to make your decision? [Listen]


Based on what you've told me, here's what differentiates my approach: [specific differentiators related to their criteria].


What questions can I answer that would help you with your comparison?"

"I NEED TO TALK TO [BOSS/PARTNER/BOARD]"

What's really being said: "I'm not the sole decision maker" (you should have discovered this earlier!)

How to respond:

"Of course. What concerns or questions do you think [decision maker] will have? [Listen]


Would it be helpful if I prepared a summary document that addresses those points? Or would it make sense for me to join a call with [decision maker] to answer questions directly?


What's your timeline for having that conversation, and when should I follow up with you?"

"CAN YOU START RIGHT AWAY?" (URGENCY PRESSURE)

What's really being said: "We have an emergency" OR "We're disorganized and need someone to fix it now"

How to respond:

"I appreciate the urgency. Can you tell me more about what's driving the timeline? [Listen]


I can start on [earliest realistic date], which would allow me to [do proper discovery/planning]. Starting too quickly without proper planning usually creates bigger problems.


However, if there are specific urgent tasks we could address immediately, we could potentially do [interim solution] while we plan the full engagement. Would that address the immediate concern?"

WARNING ABOUT URGENT CLIENTS: Clients who pressure you to start immediately without proper planning are often problem clients. They'll have unrealistic expectations, unclear scope, and will likely blame you when things go wrong.

Proceed with caution. Higher urgency should mean higher fees (rush fee), not lower quality planning.

"WE DON'T HAVE BUDGET RIGHT NOW"

What's really being said: "This isn't a priority" OR "We genuinely don't have money allocated" OR "We want you to lower your price"

How to respond:

"I understand budget constraints are real. Can I ask when budget might be available for this? [Listen]


[If next quarter/year:] Let's stay in touch. I'll check back with you in [timeframe]. In the meantime, here are a couple of things you could do internally that would help when you're ready to move forward: [helpful suggestions].


[If they're looking for discount:] Unfortunately, I'm not able to lower my pricing, as it's based on the value delivered and my expertise. However, we could look at a smaller scope that fits current budget, or I can keep you in mind if I have a pilot opportunity in the future where I offer discounted rates in exchange for case study participation."

Closing the Sale Without Being Pushy

Many sales are lost not because the client isn't interested, but because the service provider never actually asked for the business.

How to ask for the business (assumptive close):

"Based on our conversation, it sounds like this is exactly what you need. Should we move forward with this? I can get the contract to you today and we could start [specific date]."

Alternative (trial close):

When they say yes:

"Excellent! Here's what happens next: I'll send the contract and invoice today. Once I receive the signed contract and initial payment, I'll schedule our kickoff meeting for [date]. You'll have everything set up and running by [completion date]. Any questions before I send everything over?"

When they hesitate:

"I'm sensing some hesitation. What concerns do you have that we haven't addressed yet? [Listen and address]"

Red Flags: Clients to Avoid

Not every potential client is a good client. Here are warning signs to watch for:

RED FLAG #1: THEY WON'T PAY A DEPOSIT

What they say: "We'll pay you when the project is done" or "We don't pay deposits"

What this means: They don't value your work, they're testing if you're desperate, or they have cash flow problems.

Your response: "I understand that's your preference, but I require a deposit to secure project dates and cover initial setup costs. This is standard practice in the industry and protects both of us. If that doesn't work for your processes, I'm probably not the right fit."

RED FLAG #2: THEY'RE RUDE OR DISMISSIVE

What they do: Talk over you, dismiss your questions, are condescending, make you wait repeatedly

What this means: This is how they'll treat you throughout the engagement.

Your response: Walk away. Politely. "I don't think we're a good fit for this project. I wish you the best in finding the right provider."

RED FLAG #3: THEY WANT WORK DONE BEFORE SIGNING CONTRACT

What they say: "Can you just do a small sample first?" or "Show us what you'd do before we commit"

What this means: They're getting free work from multiple providers or they don't intend to hire anyone.

Your response: "I'm happy to provide a detailed proposal outlining my approach, but I don't do spec work. Once we have a signed agreement, I'll begin work. Does that work for you?"

RED FLAG #4: THEY KEEP CHANGING THE SCOPE

What they do: Every conversation adds new requirements or changes direction

What this means: They don't know what they want, and you'll be chasing moving targets forever.

Your response: "I'm noticing the scope keeps evolving. Let's take a step back and clearly define what we're solving for before moving forward. Once we have clarity, I can provide an accurate proposal."

RED FLAG #5: THEY BADMOUTH PREVIOUS PROVIDERS

What they say: "Our last [service provider] was terrible" and provide excessive detail about how awful everyone has been

What this means: They'll be saying the same thing about you next year. If everyone they've worked with was "terrible," the common denominator is them.

Your response: Proceed with extreme caution. Ask detailed questions about what went wrong and what they're looking for differently this time. If they blame everyone but themselves, walk away.

RED FLAG #6: THEY PRESSURE FOR IMMEDIATE START WITHOUT PLANNING

What they say: "We need this done by Friday" (it's currently Wednesday)

What this means: Emergency on their end, which they'll make your problem. Usually poor planning, and they'll blame you if it doesn't work out.

Your response: "I understand the urgency. However, starting without proper planning usually creates bigger problems. I can start [realistic date], or we can discuss a rush fee for expedited service. What makes the most sense?"

REMEMBER: You can fire clients before you hire them. It's much easier to avoid a problem client than to extract yourself from a bad engagement.

If your gut says something is off, listen to it. You're not desperate. You're building a sustainable business. That means saying no to the wrong clients so you have capacity for the right ones.

Communication Boundaries: Training Clients to Respect Your Time

This is where most women struggle. We've been conditioned to be available, responsive, accommodating. But unlimited availability leads to burnout and resentment.

You need boundaries. Here's how to set them without losing clients.

SETTING COMMUNICATION EXPECTATIONS

Establish from the start (include in welcome packet):

  • Your working hours: "I work Monday-Friday, 9am-5pm. Emails sent outside these hours will receive a response the next business day."
  • Response time: "I respond to emails within 24 hours on business days. For urgent matters, call/text [number]."
  • Preferred communication method: "Email is best for non-urgent matters. For quick questions, text. For complex discussions, let's schedule a call."
  • Meeting schedule: "I offer scheduled update calls [frequency]. For additional meetings, please book through my calendar link."

WHAT TO DO WHEN CLIENTS VIOLATE BOUNDARIES

When they text/email outside hours expecting immediate response:

"Thanks for reaching out. I'm not available evenings/weekends, but I'll respond first thing Monday morning. If this is urgent and can't wait, please call [number]."

When they expect you to be always available:

"I maintain set working hours to ensure I deliver quality work to all my clients. I'm available [hours] and respond to emails within 24 business hours. If you need more immediate availability, we can discuss a premium support retainer."

When they want constant updates:

"I provide progress updates [frequency]. This ensures I'm spending time on actual work rather than status meetings. I'll proactively reach out if there are any issues or delays. Does this work for you?"

When they request scope creep casually:

"That's outside the original scope we agreed to. I'm happy to add it as an additional task for [price] or we can discuss it for the next project phase. Which would you prefer?"

CRITICAL TRUTH: Clients will push boundaries if you let them. Not maliciously — they just want their problem solved as quickly and cheaply as possible.

Your job is to maintain boundaries kindly but firmly. The clients who respect your boundaries become your best clients. The ones who don't become your worst.

Better to lose a boundary-pushing client than burn out trying to accommodate them.

  • Priority 2: Important but could be done tomorrow if needed
  • Priority 3: Nice to complete but not critical

Do Priority 1 first, during your best energy time. Everything else is bonus.

Scope Creep: Identifying and Stopping It

Scope creep is when the project gradually expands beyond what was originally agreed upon — usually without corresponding increase in pay.

This is how good projects become money-losing nightmares.

HOW SCOPE CREEP HAPPENS

Subtle examples:

  • "While you're in there, could you just..."
  • "One more quick change..."
  • "I know we said 3 rounds of revisions, but..."
  • "This is related to the project, so could you..."
  • "Can you attend this meeting to explain your work?"

Each seems small. Together, they add hours of unpaid work.

PREVENTING SCOPE CREEP

1. Crystal clear scope in contract:

  • Specific deliverables listed
  • Number of revision rounds stated
  • What's NOT included explicitly listed
  • Process for handling additional requests

2. Document everything:

  • All client requests in writing
  • All agreed changes in writing
  • Regular project status updates referencing original scope

3. Create change order process:

Include in your contract:

"Changes to project scope must be requested in writing and will be assessed for additional time and cost. Change orders will be provided for approval before additional work begins."

ADDRESSING SCOPE CREEP IN THE MOMENT

When client requests something outside scope:

"I'd be happy to help with that. However, that's outside our original scope which covered [original deliverables]. I can add this as an additional task for [price] or we can discuss including it in a future project phase. Which would you prefer?"

When they push back with "it's just a small thing":

"I understand it seems small, but these additions accumulate quickly and affect my ability to deliver quality work to all clients on time. I'm happy to include it for [reasonable additional fee] or we can discuss it for the next project. What works best for you?"

When they're genuinely surprised it's extra:

"I can see how that might have seemed included. Let me show you the original scope we agreed to: [reference contract]. What you're asking for is [explain why it's different]. Moving forward, I can help you with this for [price]. Does that work?"

REMEMBER: Every time you say yes to unpaid scope creep, you're training the client that your boundaries don't matter.

Hold the line. Professional clients understand and respect this. Problematic clients will complain — let them. They're not worth losing money over.

HANDLING MISTAKES

You will make mistakes. Everyone does. How you handle them defines your professionalism.

When you catch an error before delivery:

  • Fix it
  • Add check to prevent it in future
  • Don't mention it to client (they don't need to know about problems they never experienced)

When client catches an error:

"You're absolutely right, I missed that. I apologize for the error. I'll fix it immediately and have the corrected version to you by [specific time]. I'm also adding a review step to my process to prevent this in future."

Don't:

  • Make excuses
  • Blame the client
  • Minimize the error
  • Get defensive

Do:

  • Acknowledge it
  • Apologize sincerely
  • Fix it fast
  • Explain how you'll prevent it in future
  • Consider offering small gesture of goodwill if error was significant

Avoiding Burnout: Sustainability Over Speed

You didn't leave employment to work 60-hour weeks. Protect yourself:

  • Take one full day off per week (minimum)
  • Take actual lunch breaks
  • Build in buffer time between projects

  • Raise rates before adding more hours

SIGNS YOU'RE HEADING TOWARD BURNOUT

  • Working evenings and weekends regularly
  • Resenting clients
  • Declining health or relationships
  • Quality of work dropping
  • Can't remember last day off

If you see these signs, stop and reassess:

Build for sustainability, not just survival.

Liberation Business Startup Kit - Chapter 8

Chapter 8: Growth & Scaling

The Question Nobody Asks: Do You Even Want to Scale?

Every business book, podcast, and guru will tell you that growth is the goal. Scale. Expand. Build an empire. Hire a team. Become a multi-million dollar business.

But here's the question they never ask: Is that what you actually want?

You didn't start this business to build an empire. You started it to escape systematic undervaluation and control your own income. Maybe that means staying solo and earning $100K-150K doing excellent work for 10-15 great clients. Maybe that means building a $500K business with a small team. Maybe it means something else entirely.

There's no single right answer. What matters is building the business that serves your life — not sacrificing your life to serve the business.

IMPORTANT DISTINCTION:

Growth = Earning more, working smarter, improving your business
Scaling = Systematically increasing revenue without proportionally increasing your time

You can grow without scaling. You can scale without growing into something you don't want. The choice is yours.

Growth Phase 1: Optimizing Solo Operations (Year 1-2)

Before you think about scaling, optimize what you're already doing.

RAISING YOUR RATES (THE EASIEST GROWTH STRATEGY)

The fastest way to increase income without increasing hours: charge more.

When to raise rates:

  • You're fully booked and turning away work
  • You haven't raised rates in 12+ months
  • Your skills/expertise have increased significantly
  • You've added certifications or qualifications
  • Market rates have increased
  • Your cost of living has increased
  • You're resentful about what you're earning (sign you're underpricing)

How much to raise:

  • Annual inflation increase: 5-10%
  • Skill upgrade: 15-25%
  • Major qualification/certification: 20-30%
  • Market correction (were significantly underpriced): 30-50%

How to implement rate increases:

For new clients: Just use new rates. They don't know what you used to charge.

For existing clients (30-60 days notice):

"I wanted to give you advance notice that my rates will be increasing to [new rate] effective [date]. This reflects my growing expertise, increased demand for my services, and the enhanced value I'm now able to provide.


I truly value our working relationship and wanted to give you time to plan for this change. If you have any questions, I'm happy to discuss.


Thank you for your continued trust in my services."

What usually happens:

  • 80-90% of clients accept without question
  • 5-10% ask questions but ultimately stay
  • 5-10% leave (usually your least profitable/most demanding clients)

The clients you lose are typically replaced by higher-paying clients at your new rate.

COMMON FEAR: "If I raise rates, I'll lose all my clients!"

REALITY: Good clients understand rates increase. If 10-15% increase causes mass exodus, you had the wrong clients or were severely underpricing.

Better to lose low-value clients and replace them with clients who pay appropriately than stay trapped at unsustainable rates.

INCREASING EFFICIENCY (EARNING MORE IN LESS TIME)

How to do the same work in less time:

  • Create templates: Emails, proposals, contracts, deliverables, processes
  • Batch similar tasks: All client calls on same day, all admin on Friday afternoon
  • Automate repetitive tasks: Scheduling (Calendly), invoicing (recurring), email sequences
  • Document your processes: Create SOPs so you're not reinventing the wheel each time
  • Invest in better tools: Software that saves time is worth the cost
  • Eliminate low-value activities: Stop doing things that don't generate income or improve service

Example efficiency gains:

  • Contract templates + e-signature: Saves 2 hours per client
  • Email templates for common responses: Saves 5 hours per week
  • Automated scheduling: Saves 3 hours per week
  • Project management system: Saves 4 hours per week
  • Batching similar work: 20% time savings through focus

Result: Work that took 40 hours now takes 30 hours. Same income, less time, or fill those 10 hours with higher-value work.

MOVING FROM HOURLY TO VALUE-BASED PRICING

If you're still charging hourly, transitioning to value-based or project pricing is the single biggest growth lever.

Why this matters:

  • Hourly pricing caps your income (finite hours available)
  • You're penalized for getting faster/better
  • Value pricing aligns your interests with client outcomes
  • Projects that took 10 hours last year now take 6 — but value hasn't decreased

How to transition existing hourly clients:

"I'm moving to project-based pricing because I've found it provides better clarity for both of us and allows me to focus on results rather than hours. For your next project, instead of hourly billing, I'll provide a fixed project fee of [amount] for [specific deliverables]. This gives you cost certainty and ensures I'm incentivized to deliver efficiently rather than slowly."

NICHING DOWN (COUNTERINTUITIVE BUT EFFECTIVE)

When you're worried about growth, the instinct is to broaden your services. The reality? Specialization often leads to faster growth.

Benefits of niching:

  • Command higher rates (specialist premium)
  • Attract better clients (they seek specialists)
  • Easier marketing (clear message to specific audience)
  • Faster delivery (you've solved this problem before)
  • Stronger referrals (easy to explain who you help)

Ways to niche:

  • Industry: "I only work with legal firms" vs "I work with all businesses"
  • Problem: "I only do process documentation" vs "I do everything admin-related"
  • Client type: "I work with solo consultants scaling to small teams" vs "Anyone"
  • Method: "I specialize in Excel-based solutions" vs "I use whatever"

Example transformation:

  • Before niche: "I do bookkeeping for small businesses" — $50/hour, competing with everyone
  • After niche: "I do bookkeeping specifically for allied health practices" — $120/hour, seen as specialist who understands their unique needs

Growth Phase 2: Leveraging Your Time (Year 2-3)

You've optimized operations, raised rates, and maybe niched down. Now you're hitting a ceiling: there are only so many hours you can work.

Options for breaking through this ceiling:

OPTION 1: PRODUCTIZED SERVICES

What it is: Turn your custom service into a standardized package with fixed scope and pricing.

Example: Instead of "custom bookkeeping services," offer:

  • "Bookkeeping Cleanup Package: $2,500 — Get 12 months of messy books cleaned up and reconciled in 2 weeks"
  • "Monthly Bookkeeping: $800/month — Standard package for businesses under $500K revenue"

Benefits:

  • Easier to sell (client knows exactly what they're getting)
  • Faster to deliver (you've done it before)
  • Higher margins (efficiency from repetition)
  • Can potentially be delegated

How to create a productized service:

  • Identify your most common client request
  • Standardize the scope and deliverables
  • Create a process/checklist for delivery
  • Set fixed pricing
  • Market it as a package, not custom work

OPTION 2: GROUP PROGRAMS OR WORKSHOPS

What it is: Serve multiple clients simultaneously through training, workshops, or group consulting.

Examples:

  • Monthly group coaching call ($200/month per person, 10 people = $2,000 for 2 hours)
  • Workshop: "Financial Systems for Growing Businesses" ($500 per attendee, 12 attendees = $6,000 for 1 day)
  • Online course: "DIY Bookkeeping Setup" ($300, sell 10 per month = $3,000 passive-ish income)

Benefits:

  • One-to-many model (leverage time)
  • Higher effective hourly rate
  • Builds authority and visibility
  • Can lead to 1-on-1 client relationships

Challenges:

  • Requires marketing and audience building
  • Upfront time investment to create
  • Not everyone wants group vs. individual attention

OPTION 3: RETAINER CLIENTS

What it is: Ongoing monthly fee for continued access to your services.

Structure options:

  • Hours-based: "10 hours per month for $2,500" (but watch out for hour-tracking trap)
  • Access-based: "$2,000/month for ongoing support, priority response, monthly strategy session"
  • Deliverables-based: "$1,500/month includes weekly reports, monthly reconciliation, quarterly planning"

Benefits:

  • Predictable recurring revenue
  • Deeper client relationships
  • Easier to plan your time
  • Less sales effort (one sale = ongoing income)

How to transition project clients to retainer:

"I've really enjoyed working on [project] with you. Given that you'll likely need ongoing support with [service area], I wanted to offer you a retainer arrangement. For $[amount] per month, you'd get [specific deliverables/access]. This gives you consistent support without having to request and approve individual projects each time. Would this be valuable for you?"

OPTION 4: PREMIUM/VIP OFFERING

What it is: High-touch, high-price offering for clients who want premium service.

Examples:

  • "VIP Day: $3,500 — Intensive full-day session to solve [specific problem]"
  • "Platinum Package: $10,000 — Everything in standard package + priority access, weekly calls, custom training"
  • "White-glove service: 2x standard rate for same-day response, after-hours availability, priority scheduling"

Benefits:

  • Significantly higher revenue per client
  • Attract clients who value premium service
  • You choose your best clients (can be selective at this price point)

Who buys premium:

  • Clients with urgent problems
  • Clients who value convenience over cost
  • Established businesses with healthy budgets
  • Clients who've worked with you before and trust you

Growth Phase 3: Building a Team (Year 3+)

This is where most people think "scaling" begins. But adding team members is a major decision that fundamentally changes your business.

BEFORE YOU HIRE: THE HONEST ASSESSMENT

Good reasons to hire:

  • You're consistently turning away good clients due to capacity
  • Specific tasks are preventing you from higher-value work
  • You have enough revenue to support additional salary + overhead
  • You want to build a larger business (not just earn more personally)
  • You have systems and processes documented

Bad reasons to hire:

  • You're overwhelmed but it's due to poor systems, not lack of capacity
  • You think it will impress people
  • You want someone else to do the work you don't like (but haven't optimized)
  • You're not yet profitable as a solo operator
  • You don't have reliable recurring revenue

REALITY CHECK: Hiring someone doesn't just add their work output. It adds:

▸ Management time (training, oversight, communication)
▸ Administrative overhead (payroll, leave management, performance reviews)
▸ Legal obligations (employment law, workplace safety, contracts)
▸ Financial risk (ongoing salary commitment regardless of revenue)
▸ Stress (now responsible for someone else's livelihood)

Many solo business owners earn more and have less stress than those with small teams.

HIRING OPTIONS: CHOOSING THE RIGHT MODEL

Option 1: Virtual Assistant (VA) or Contractor

Best for: Administrative tasks, specific projects, overflow work

Structure:

  • Hired as contractor, not employee
  • Pay per hour or per project
  • No employment obligations (super, leave, etc.)
  • Can increase/decrease hours as needed

Cost: $25-60/hour depending on skill level and location

What to delegate:

  • Email management and calendar scheduling
  • Social media posting
  • Basic bookkeeping data entry
  • Client onboarding administration
  • Document formatting and organization

Option 2: Specialist Contractor/Subcontractor

Best for: Specific skilled work you can delegate

Structure:

  • Contractor with specific expertise
  • You manage client relationship, they deliver specific work
  • Pay per project or revenue share

Cost: $50-150/hour or 40-60% of project fee

Example: You sell bookkeeping services, subcontractor does the actual bookkeeping while you manage client relationships and business development

Option 3: Part-Time Employee

Best for: Consistent ongoing work, need for reliability

Structure:

  • Employment contract
  • Regular hours (e.g., 20 hours/week)
  • Super, leave entitlements, WorkCover insurance
  • More control and commitment than contractor

Cost: Salary + 11% super + 20% leave loading = approximately 140% of base salary

Option 4: Full-Time Employee

Best for: Established business with consistent high revenue

When you're ready:

  • $200K+ annual revenue minimum
  • 6+ months operating expenses in reserve
  • Stable client base
  • Clear role that requires 35+ hours/week

True cost: $60K salary = approximately $84K total cost (super, leave, WorkCover, payroll tax if applicable)

The practical parts of business — finding clients, delivering work, managing money — those are learnable. The mental and emotional challenges of unlearning decades of conditioning that told you to be small, grateful, and inexpensive? That's the real work.

This chapter won't fix it completely. But it will give you tools to fight back when your brain tries to sabotage your success.

Imposter Syndrome: Why It Hits Women Harder

Imposter syndrome is the persistent feeling that you're not actually qualified, that you're faking it, that any success is luck or deception rather than earned competence.

Everyone experiences some version of this. But women who've been systematically underpaid face a specific, insidious variant:

You were told for decades that you weren't worth what men were paid. Now you're charging professional rates and your brain is screaming that this confirms you were never worth it in the first place.

WHY IMPOSTER SYNDROME IS WORSE FOR UNDERPAID WOMEN

You have "evidence" you're not good enough:

Your brain says: "If you were actually valuable, you would have been paid fairly all those years."

Reality: You were undervalued due to systemic discrimination, not lack of competence.

You've internalized others' undervaluation:

Your brain says: "Multiple employers paid you less. They can't all be wrong."

Reality: Systemic bias is consistent, not correct.

You lack comparison points for "normal":

Your brain says: "Charging $150/hour feels outrageous."

Reality: It feels outrageous because you were paid $30/hour for the same work. The $150 is market rate; the $30 was exploitation.

You were trained to be grateful, not confident:

Your brain says: "I should be grateful anyone pays me, not asking for more."

Reality: Business transactions are exchanges of value, not charity. Gratitude is for gifts. This is commerce.

WHAT IMPOSTER SYNDROME SOUNDS LIKE

  • "I'm not qualified enough to charge this much"
  • "They're going to realize I don't know what I'm doing"
  • "I got lucky with these clients; it won't last"
  • "Someone with actual credentials should be doing this, not me"
  • "I'm just really good at faking confidence"
  • "When they find out who I really am, this ends"
  • "I shouldn't be succeeding when more qualified people are struggling"

HOW TO FIGHT BACK

1. Evidence Collection

Create a "Proof File" — physical or digital folder containing:

  • Client testimonials and thank-you messages
  • Evidence of results you've delivered
  • Payment confirmations (proof people willingly paid your rates)
  • Referrals you've received
  • Qualifications, certifications, years of experience
  • Problems you've solved

When imposter syndrome strikes, review this file. Your feelings are lying to you. The evidence is truth.

2. Reframe the Narrative

THOUGHT CHALLENGING EXERCISE:


Imposter thought: "I'm not qualified to charge $120/hour"


Evidence against this thought:
▸ I have 15 years of experience doing this work
▸ Three clients have paid this rate without hesitation
▸ I consistently deliver results clients are happy with
▸ Market research shows this is standard for my expertise
▸ I have testimonials proving my value


Reframed thought: "I'm qualified and experienced. This rate reflects my actual market value, not my internalized undervaluation."

3. Separate Feelings from Facts

Imposter syndrome is a feeling, not a fact. Practice saying:

  • "I feel like a fraud" (accurate — this is how you feel)
  • NOT "I am a fraud" (this is a conclusion, likely false)

Feelings are data about your internal state, not objective reality about your competence.

4. Acknowledge It and Move Forward Anyway

You don't have to eliminate imposter syndrome to succeed. You just have to not let it stop you.

"I feel like I'm not qualified to charge this rate. I'm going to charge it anyway because the evidence shows I deliver value worth this price."


"I feel like they're going to realize I'm a fraud. I'm going to deliver excellent work anyway because that's what I do."


"I feel like I don't deserve this success. I'm going to accept it anyway because feelings aren't facts."

The Fear Inventory: Naming What's Actually Scaring You

Fear is normal when you're building something. But vague, unnamed fear paralyzes. Specific, identified fear can be addressed.

COMMON BUSINESS FEARS (AND THEIR TRUTHS)

Fear: "I'll fail and lose everything"

Truth: You're testing a business idea, not betting your life savings on roulette. Worst case: you go back to employment with new skills and experience. That's not devastation; it's a valuable learning experience.

Action: Define your actual worst case. Write it down. Is it survivable? Usually yes.

Fear: "Nobody will hire me"

Truth: Businesses have problems. You solve problems. If you can communicate the connection, someone will hire you. Maybe not everyone. But someone.

Action: You only need a handful of clients to start. Not thousands. Just a few.

Fear: "I'll succeed and then fail publicly"

Truth: Very few people are paying attention to your business journey. Those who are watching closely are usually rooting for you, not waiting for you to fail.

Action: Ask yourself: whose opinion actually matters? Usually a very short list.

Fear: "I don't know enough"

Truth: You know more than people who haven't done this work for 10-20 years. You don't need to know everything; you need to know more than your clients about your specific area of expertise.

Action: List what you DO know. It's longer than you think.

Fear: "I'll be exposed as not good enough"

Action: Create a plan for handling mistakes. Having a plan reduces anxiety.

Fear: "I'll succeed and people will resent me"

Truth: Some people might. Those people aren't your people. The right people celebrate your success.

Action: Surround yourself with people who want you to succeed, not those who need you to stay small.

Decision Fatigue and Analysis Paralysis

As a business owner, you make dozens of decisions daily. This is exhausting, especially when you've been conditioned to seek approval rather than trust your own judgment.

THE PERMISSION PROBLEM

For decades, you asked permission:

  • Permission to take a day off
  • Permission to work differently
  • Permission to earn more
  • Permission to have boundaries

Now nobody is giving you permission. You have to give it to yourself. And that feels terrifying and illegitimate.

The fix: You are the permission. When you catch yourself waiting for approval that will never come, say out loud: "I give myself permission to [make this decision, set this boundary, charge this rate, try this approach]."

For small decisions (under $100 or 2 hours of time):

Make them quickly. Don't deliberate. Pick an option and move on. Most small decisions are reversible.

For medium decisions ($100-1000 or significant time):

BUILDING RESILIENCE

Resilience isn't about not falling. It's about how quickly you get back up.

Practices that build resilience:

  • Regular review of wins: Keep a "wins journal" — write down every success, no matter how small
  • Separate identity from outcomes: "I had a bad month" not "I am a failure"
  • Time-bound emotions: "I'm disappointed today about losing that client" not "I'll always be terrible at this"
  • Action focus: "What's my next move?" instead of "Why did this happen to me?"
  • Perspective: "In 5 years, will this matter?" (Usually no)

The Confidence Paradox

Here's the paradox: you think you need confidence before you act. Actually, you build confidence by acting despite lack of confidence.

Confidence doesn't come from thinking positive thoughts or affirmations. It comes from evidence that you can do hard things.

THE CONFIDENCE BUILDING CYCLE:


1. Do something scary despite fear (send proposal, raise rates, set boundary)
2. Survive it (it probably goes better than feared)
3. Gather evidence of competence (they paid, they accepted, they respected it)
4. Next time is slightly less scary
5. Repeat


You don't wait for confidence. You build it through accumulated evidence of doing the thing.

Every time you:

  • Send a proposal at your full rate despite fear
  • Set a boundary despite guilt
  • Say no to a bad-fit client despite scarcity mindset
  • Deliver work despite imposter syndrome
  • Handle a mistake professionally despite shame

...you build evidence that you can do this. And evidence creates confidence.

What helps:

  • Acknowledge the feeling without letting it drive decisions
  • Keep building (don't self-sabotage from fear of loss)
  • Diversify (multiple clients, income streams) so one loss isn't catastrophic
  • Save (financial buffer reduces anxiety)
  • Document systems (business survives individual client losses)

THE "I DON'T DESERVE THIS" FEELING

When you achieve something you wanted — good client, strong month, rate increase accepted — and instead of celebrating, you feel guilty or undeserving.

This is internalized undervaluation whispering that you're getting away with something, that you're taking more than you should have.

The reality: You earned this through skills, effort, and value delivered. Business transactions are exchanges, not theft. You're not "getting away with" anything.

What helps:

  • Practice receiving without apology
  • Remind yourself: clients pay willingly because they receive value
  • Notice when you minimize your success ("I just got lucky")
  • Celebrate wins, even small ones
