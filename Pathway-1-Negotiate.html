Why Negotiation Can Work (And When It Won't)

Let me start with the realistic scenarios where negotiation actually has a chance of working.

When Negotiation Can Work:

1. Your employer genuinely didn't know you were underpaid

This happens more often than you'd think. If you've been quietly doing excellent work without making noise, your manager might genuinely not realize how underpaid you are compared to market rates or compared to your colleagues. When you present the data, they might be genuinely surprised—and willing to fix it.

2. Your manager has budget authority and values you

If your direct manager can approve raises and genuinely appreciates your work, they might be able to fix your pay immediately. This works best when you have a good relationship with your manager and they have the power to make decisions without layers of approval.

3. You have leverage

If you're hard to replace, if you have specialized knowledge, if losing you would genuinely hurt the business, negotiation has more power. The more you cost them to replace, the more likely they are to pay you properly.

4. The company has money

Sounds obvious, but if the company is profitable and spending money elsewhere (hiring new people, giving executives bonuses, investing in new projects), there's budget available. Your job is to redirect some of it toward your correct salary.

5. There's a legal risk

If your underpayment is clearly discriminatory (you're paid less than men doing the same job with the same experience), your employer might fix it immediately to avoid legal exposure. Companies don't like Fair Work complaints or discrimination lawsuits.

6. You're prepared to walk

If you're genuinely ready to leave if they say no—and they know it—negotiation has teeth. Employers take you more seriously when they know you have other options and you're not bluffing.

When Negotiation Won't Work:

1. The company is struggling financially

If they're doing layoffs, cutting costs, or genuinely struggling to make payroll, there might not be money available no matter how good your case is. You can't negotiate with an empty budget.

2. Your underpayment is intentional

Some employers underpay women deliberately. They've calculated that you'll accept less, and they're banking on you not making waves. These employers won't negotiate in good faith because your low pay is working exactly as they designed it.

3. Your manager is threatened by you

If your manager sees you as competition, or if paying you fairly would highlight their own shortcomings, they'll block your raise even if they have budget. This is especially common when women are more competent than their male managers.

4. The culture is toxic

In workplaces where women are fundamentally undervalued, where sexual harassment is dismissed as "just Steve being Steve," where speaking up is punished—negotiation won't work. The underpayment isn't a mistake; it's the culture.

5. You've been "difficult"

If you've previously complained about harassment, discrimination, or unfair treatment, your employer may have already decided you're a problem. Fair or not, speaking truth to power often gets women labeled as troublemakers. Once that happens, negotiation is nearly impossible.

6. They're planning to replace you

If the company is already looking to outsource your role, hire someone cheaper, or eliminate your position, they won't invest in paying you fairly. They're just waiting for the right time to let you go.

How to Tell Which Scenario You're In:

Ask yourself honestly:

  • Does my manager regularly thank me and acknowledge my work? (Good sign)
  • Has my manager ever mentioned wanting to pay me more but being blocked? (Good sign)
  • Am I the only woman in a male-dominated workplace? (Red flag)
  • Has HR dismissed my concerns in the past? (Red flag)
  • Are new hires being paid more than me? (Red flag)
  • Is the company profitable? (Good sign)
  • Do I have specialized knowledge they can't easily replace? (Good sign)
  • Have I complained about unfair treatment before? (Complicating factor)

REALITY CHECK: "I've been with this company 15 years" is not leverage. Loyalty means nothing if they don't value you. Time served doesn't justify higher pay—performance and market value do. If they've underpaid you for 15 years, they're not going to suddenly develop a conscience.

The truth is, you won't know for certain whether negotiation will work until you try. That's what this kit is for—to help you try strategically, with documentation, so that whether they say yes or no, you're protected and informed.

Your Number is Not Negotiable

You've calculated your underpayment number. Maybe it's $75,000 over 10 years. Maybe it's $300,000 over 20 years. Maybe, like me, it's over a million.

That number is real. It's based on market rates for the work you actually did, not the understated title they gave you. It's based on what men in comparable roles were earning while you were earning less.

Here's what you need to understand: You're not asking for a favour. You're asking for correction of an injustice.

When you go into this negotiation, you're not begging. You're not hoping they'll be generous. You're presenting evidence that you've been systematically underpaid and requesting that they fix it going forward.

Now, I'm realistic. You're probably not going to get back pay for every dollar you lost. Most employers won't agree to that, and legally they may not have to (we'll cover the legal basics in Section 8).

But what you CAN negotiate is:

  • Immediate salary correction to market rate (or close to it)
  • Title correction to reflect what you actually do
  • Back pay for a defined recent period (6-12 months is more realistic than 10 years)
  • Bonus or lump sum to acknowledge past underpayment
  • Benefits improvement (more annual leave, professional development budget, flexibility)
  • Defined review timeline (another review in 6 months, not "maybe next year")

What "Market Rate" Actually Means

Market rate is NOT:

  • What they currently pay you
  • What they paid the last person in your role
  • What they think the role is worth
  • What their budget allows

Market rate IS:

  • What companies in your industry pay for your actual responsibilities
  • What men with your experience level earn doing comparable work
  • What job ads are offering for roles like yours
  • What recruiters tell you your skills command

Here's the critical mindset shift: You're not negotiating what you think you can get. You're negotiating for what the work is actually worth.

If your research shows that operations managers with your experience in your city earn $85,000-$95,000, and you're making $60,000, then $85,000 is your starting point. Not $65,000. Not $70,000. Not "just a little more."

Why? Because anything less than market rate means you're STILL underpaid. You're just less underpaid than before.

The "Grateful" Trap

Women are taught to be grateful for any increase. A manager offers you $5,000 more and expects you to be thrilled. You probably will be thrilled—$5,000 is real money, especially when you've been struggling.

But if that $5,000 still leaves you $20,000 below market rate, you're still being underpaid. The company is still benefiting from your undervaluation. They just paid you enough to stop you from leaving—not enough to be fair.

Don't negotiate against yourself by lowering your expectations before you even start.

I did this. Every time. I'd research market rates, see I should be earning $85,000, but then I'd convince myself that $70,000 would be "good enough" because it was more than I had. I'd go into negotiations asking for $70,000 instead of $85,000.

Guess what? They'd counter with $65,000, and I'd accept it, grateful for the $5,000 increase. Meanwhile, I was still $20,000 underpaid.

Your number—the real market rate—is not negotiable in your own mind. You might not get it from this employer. They might refuse. But don't reduce it before you even ask.

ACTION STEP: Write down the market rate for your role right now. That's your target. Not a dream number. Not a "wouldn't it be nice" number. The actual market rate. That's what you're negotiating for.

Common Negotiation Mistakes Women Make (And How to Avoid Them)

I've made every mistake on this list. Some of them multiple times. Learn from my expensive errors:

Mistake #1: Apologizing for Asking

What it sounds like:

  • "I'm sorry to bring this up, but..."
  • "I hate to be a bother, but..."
  • "I know this is awkward, but..."
  • "I hope you don't mind me asking, but..."

Why it's a problem: You're signaling that you think asking for fair pay is wrong. You're apologizing for existing. Men don't do this. They walk in and state what they want.

What to do instead: State your request directly and professionally:

  • "I'd like to discuss my compensation."
  • "I'm requesting a salary review based on market data."
  • "I've prepared a case for a salary adjustment and I'd like to present it."

No apologies. You're not asking for a favour—you're addressing a business issue.

Mistake #2: Accepting the First Number They Offer

What it sounds like: Manager: "We can offer you a $3,000 increase." You: "Oh, thank you so much! That's great!"

Why it's a problem: If they're offering something, they have budget. The first offer is never their best offer. They're testing to see if you'll accept less.

What to do instead: Acknowledge, pause, and counter:

"I appreciate the offer. Based on my research showing market rate for this role is $X, I was expecting a figure closer to that. Can we discuss bridging that gap?"

Even if their first offer seems generous, pause. Thank them. Then ask if there's room for more. You'll be surprised how often there is.

Mistake #3: Justifying the Request with Personal Needs

What it sounds like:

  • "I really need this raise because my rent just went up."
  • "My car needs repairs and I can't afford it."
  • "My daughter is starting university and I need help with costs."

Why it's a problem: Personal financial stress is not a business case. Employers pay for value delivered, not for your cost of living. When you make it about your needs, you're begging, not negotiating.

What to do instead: Make it about business value:

  • "My responsibilities have expanded significantly beyond my original job description."
  • "Market rate for this role with my experience level is $X, and I'm currently at $Y."
  • "I've delivered measurable results including [specific achievements with dollar impact]."

Your personal situation is real and valid, but it's not their concern. Focus on what you deliver.

Mistake #4: Accepting Vague Promises

What it sounds like: Manager: "We'll look at it in the next budget cycle." Manager: "I'll see what I can do." Manager: "We're planning some restructuring and we'll address it then." Manager: "I definitely want to get you more money, just give me some time."

Why it's a problem: Vague promises are how they delay without committing. "I'll try" means "no." "We'll look at it later" means "I'm hoping you forget."

What to do instead: Pin them down:

  • "I appreciate that. Can we set a specific date for the follow-up conversation? I'd like to revisit this on [specific date]."
  • "What specific actions are you taking between now and then?"
  • "Can we document this commitment in writing so we're both clear on the timeline?"

Get dates. Get commitments. Get it in writing.

Mistake #5: Providing Too Much Information

What it sounds like: You spend 30 minutes explaining every single task you do, every project you've worked on, every person you've helped, talking faster and faster, trying to justify why you deserve more money...

Why it's a problem: Information overload dilutes your message. They stop listening. It also signals that you're not confident in your case—you're throwing everything at the wall hoping something sticks.

What to do instead: Use the three-part structure (we'll cover this in Section 4):

  • Here's the market rate data (2 minutes)
  • Here's evidence I deliver at that level (2 minutes)
  • Here's what I'm requesting (1 minute)

Crisp. Clear. Confident. Stop talking. Let them respond.

Mistake #6: Negotiating with the Wrong Person

What it sounds like: You spend weeks building your case, preparing your data, rehearsing your script... and then you present it to someone who has no authority to approve it.

Why it's a problem: You've wasted your preparation and shown your hand to someone who can't help you. Now they'll report back to the decision-maker, who will have time to prepare counterarguments.

What to do instead: Identify who actually makes compensation decisions:

  • Is it your direct manager?
  • Is it their manager?
  • Is it HR?
  • Is there a committee?

Start with your direct manager but ask explicitly: "Do you have authority to approve salary adjustments, or would this need to go to [next level]?"

If they don't have authority, ask them to advocate for you and request a meeting with the person who does.

Mistake #7: Accepting Non-Monetary Compensation as a Substitute

What it sounds like: Manager: "I can't give you a raise right now, but I can offer you a flexible work arrangement." Manager: "How about a fancier title instead?" Manager: "I can give you more professional development opportunities."

Why it's a problem: These things have value, but they don't pay your bills. They don't close your wage gap. They don't build your superannuation. Companies offer these when they don't want to pay you more money.

What to do instead: Acknowledge the offer but stay focused:

"I appreciate the flexibility/title/development offer, and I'd welcome that IN ADDITION to the salary adjustment we're discussing. However, the core issue is that my current salary is below market rate for this role, and I need that addressed."

Take the extras if they're offered, but don't accept them instead of money.

Mistake #8: Believing "Budget Constraints" Without Evidence

What it sounds like: Manager: "I'd love to pay you more, but we just don't have the budget." You: "Oh, I understand. Thank you for considering it."

Why it's a problem: "No budget" is the easiest excuse to give. It's often not true. Companies find budget for what they prioritize. I was told "no budget" the week before the boss purchased a $220,000 Range Rover.

What to do instead: Probe gently:

  • "I understand budget is a concern. Can you help me understand the budget process? When would funds potentially become available?"
  • "Are there any roles currently being hired where budget is available? I'm curious about the prioritization."
  • "If budget became available, would my request be approved?"

If they're hiring new people, giving bonuses to executives, or spending on other initiatives, budget exists. It's a prioritization issue, not a resource issue.

Mistake #9: Getting Emotional

What it sounds like: You start crying. Or your voice shakes. Or you get angry and raise your voice. Or you say things like "This is so unfair!" or "I can't believe you're doing this to me!"

Why it's a problem: Fair or not, women who show emotion in professional settings are dismissed. You'll be labeled "unstable" or "too emotional" or "not professional enough." Your valid points will be ignored because they'll focus on how you delivered them.

What to do instead: This is brutally hard, but: stay calm. If you feel tears coming, pause and take a breath. If you're getting angry, pause and take a breath.

Practice this script: "I need a moment. Can we take a brief break?" Then step out, compose yourself, and return.

If you do cry (it happens—I've done it), acknowledge it briefly and move on: "I apologize for getting emotional. This is important to me. Let me restate my request clearly." Then continue with your case.

Mistake #10: Staying After They've Shown You Who They Are

What it sounds like: They refuse your fair request. You stay anyway. You convince yourself it will get better. You wait for the next review cycle. You accept another vague promise. You stay for years, hoping they'll eventually value you.

Why it's a problem: When an employer clearly demonstrates they won't pay you fairly, believe them. If you present solid data, deliver excellent work, and they still refuse to correct your pay, they've told you everything you need to know about how they value you.

What to do instead: If they refuse your well-documented request for market-rate pay, start planning your exit. You might not leave tomorrow, but you should activate Path 2 (Strategic Exit). Update your resume. Start networking. Begin looking.

Staying after a clear refusal means accepting ongoing underpayment. Don't do what I did—don't stay for another decade hoping things will change.

If you're split between a support role (1-3) and a strategic role (4-10):
→ Use the STRATEGIC role number. Strategic/managerial work is more valuable. Research that salary range.

If you're split between Coordinator (4 or 6) and Manager (5 or 7):

If you're split evenly between two equally strategic roles:
→ Research BOTH roles. Use the HIGHER salary range. You're providing more value by being multi-skilled.

→ You're severely overworked and underpaid. Use the HIGHEST role level you scored significantly on (at least 6+ boxes checked). That's your real role.

Location Matters: Major Cities vs. Regional

Salaries vary significantly by location in Australia. Generally:

  • Sydney and Melbourne pay highest (10-20% above national average)
  • Perth, Brisbane, Adelaide pay slightly above or at national average
  • Regional areas pay 10-15% below metro averages

However, if you work for a national company or in a role that's location-independent, you should be paid based on the role's value, not just local rates.

Adjusting for Your Location

Capital city salary patterns:

  • 5-10% below Sydney/Melbourne for equivalent roles
  • On par with or slightly above Brisbane
  • 15-20% above regional WA

If your research is showing national averages, you can reasonably adjust Perth rates to be about 5% below the national average IF the national average is heavily weighted toward Sydney/Melbourne.

Example:

  • National average for Operations Manager: $90,000
  • Your city-adjusted rate: Calculate based on your location's premium/discount

However, if you work for a multinational company, a government agency, or in mining/resources sector, Perth rates are often comparable to eastern states due to the cost of living and the specific industry dynamics.

When Location Shouldn't Matter

Don't accept a location-based pay reduction if:

  • Your company is national/international (they should have consistent pay bands)
  • Your role could be done remotely from anywhere
  • You're being compared to employees in other locations doing the same work
  • Cost of living in Perth is comparable (and it is—Perth isn't cheap)

REALITY CHECK: Companies love to use "location-based pay" as an excuse to pay less, especially to women. If they're hiring someone in Sydney for $100K but offering you $75K in Perth for the same role, that's not location adjustment—that's exploitation.

What Counts as Evidence

Market research shows you SHOULD be paid more. Evidence shows you DESERVE to be paid more.

You need both.

Here's what counts as strong evidence in a pay negotiation:

✅ STRONG EVIDENCE:

1. Expanded Responsibilities

  • Tasks or projects you're doing now that weren't in your original job description
  • Staff or contractors you're supervising (especially if supervision wasn't originally in your role)
  • Budgets you're managing
  • Systems or processes you've implemented
  • Additional roles you've absorbed (often when someone left and their work was dumped on you)

2. Measurable Achievements

  • Cost savings you've generated
  • Revenue increases you've contributed to
  • Time savings from process improvements
  • Error reduction or quality improvements
  • Successful project completions
  • Client satisfaction improvements
  • Efficiency gains (quantified)

3. Performance Reviews

  • Positive performance reviews (especially specific comments about exceeding expectations)
  • Awards or recognition
  • Commendations from clients or stakeholders
  • Thank-you emails from senior management

4. Increased Scope

  • More complex work than when you started
  • Increased number of stakeholders you support
  • Larger projects under your management
  • Additional locations or teams you support

5. Certifications or Training

  • Professional certifications you've earned
  • Training programs you've completed
  • Skills you've developed that add value

❌ WEAK EVIDENCE (Don't rely on these alone):

1. Tenure

"I've been here 10 years" is not evidence of increased value. It's evidence of loyalty, which employers don't reward unless it comes with performance.

2. Personal Needs

"My rent went up" or "I have family obligations" are not business cases.

3. Comparison Without Data

"I think I do more than Sarah" isn't useful unless you can specifically articulate what you do that Sarah doesn't.

4. General Statements

"I work really hard" or "I always go above and beyond" are too vague. You need specifics.

5. Promises Made

"You said you'd review my salary last year" might be true, but it's not evidence of your value—it's evidence of their broken promise. (Document it, but don't lead with it.)

How to Calculate Dollar Impact When It's Not Obvious:

1. Time Savings:

Hours saved × Hourly rate of people affected = Dollar value

Example: Process improvement saves 2 hours per week across 5 staff
2 hours × 5 people × 50 weeks = 500 hours saved
500 hours × $50 average hourly rate = $25,000 annual value

2. Error Reduction:

How many errors before vs. after your improvement?
What's the average cost to fix each error? (Time + resources)

Example: Reduced data entry errors from 50/month to 10/month
40 fewer errors × 1 hour to correct each × $50 hourly rate = $2,000 monthly savings = $24,000 annually

3. Cost Savings:

Old cost - New cost = Savings

Example: Negotiated better vendor rates, switched suppliers, eliminated waste
Old annual cost: $50,000
New annual cost: $42,000
Savings: $8,000 annually

4. Revenue Support:

Did you support a project or team that generated revenue?

Example: "Provided operations support for the [Project Name] initiative that delivered $2M in new contracts"
You're not claiming you generated $2M, but you're showing you were essential to the team that did

5. Risk Mitigation:

Did you prevent problems that would have cost money?

Example: "Identified compliance issue before audit, avoiding potential fine of $50,000"
Example: "Implemented backup system that prevented data loss during server outage, protecting approximately $100,000 in client deliverables"

6. Quality Improvements:

Reduced complaints, improved satisfaction, decreased rework

Example: "Improved customer satisfaction scores from 72% to 89%, reducing customer churn by an estimated 15%, representing approximately $75,000 in retained revenue annually"

7. Efficiency Gains:

Process that took X time now takes Y time

Example: "Automated monthly reporting process, reducing preparation time from 16 hours to 3 hours, saving 13 hours monthly (156 hours annually) valued at $7,800"

Coming up in Section 4: These dollar-impact statements become powerful ammunition when addressing budget concerns or proving your ROI to the organization. Your manager can't argue with concrete numbers.

When to Ask: Timing Matters

Timing can make the difference between success and failure in a negotiation. Here's when to ask:

✅ GOOD TIMING:

After a significant achievement
You just completed a major project, resolved a crisis, or delivered exceptional results. Strike while your value is visible.

During performance review cycle
If your company does annual reviews, this is the expected time to discuss compensation. You're not catching anyone off guard.

When you have a job offer elsewhere
If you're genuinely considering leaving and have another offer, that's maximum leverage. (But don't bluff—only use this if you're truly prepared to leave.)

After taking on significant new responsibilities
When your role expands substantially, that's the time to address compensation. Don't wait months or years.

When the company is doing well
If the company just announced record profits, landed a major contract, or received significant funding, budget is available.

After completing research and documentation
When you've done all the work in Sections 2-4 of this kit. Don't rush in unprepared.

Early in the budget cycle
If your company's financial year runs July-June, ask in July-September (early budget cycle), not May-June (budget already allocated).

❌ BAD TIMING:

During company crisis
If there are layoffs, financial struggles, or major operational disruptions, you'll get an automatic no.

Right after making a major mistake
If you just messed up a project or received negative feedback, wait until you've recovered and demonstrated improvement.

When your manager is overwhelmed
If your boss is dealing with their own crisis, deadline, or personal issue, they won't give your request proper attention.

During probation period
Unless explicitly discussed, wait until you're past probationary period and have proven your value.

Right after someone was let go
If the company just did layoffs or fired people, they're not in a mindset to increase anyone's compensation.

December/end of financial year
Budgets are often locked. Better to ask in early budget planning period.

During your vacation or theirs
Don't ask via email while one of you is on leave. This needs a proper face-to-face conversation.

Friday afternoon
They won't have time to properly consider it before the weekend, and you'll spend the weekend anxious.

Optimal Timing Strategy:

Plan your ask for 2-4 weeks after a significant achievement or at the beginning of the performance review cycle, when company is financially stable, and when you're fully prepared with research and documentation.

Give yourself time to prepare properly. Don't rush this.

❌ Apologize for taking their time or for asking for fair pay

❌ Over-explain or ramble (dilutes your message)

DISCLAIMER

This is general information, not legal advice. For specific legal questions, consult an employment lawyer or Fair Work Australia.

Fair Work Act: Pay Equity Overview

In Australia, the Fair Work Act 2009 is the primary legislation governing employment relationships, including pay equity.

KEY PRINCIPLES:

1. Equal Pay for Equal Work

The Fair Work Act requires that men and women receive equal pay for equal work. This includes equal pay for work of equal or comparable value.

What this means:

  • If you're doing the same work as a male colleague, you should be paid the same
  • If you're doing work of comparable value, you should be paid comparably
  • Pay disparities based on gender are illegal

Section 302 of the Fair Work Act specifically prohibits discrimination in pay on the basis of sex.

2. National Minimum Wage

There's a national minimum wage that applies to all employees (currently $23.23/hour as of 1 July 2024, but check Fair Work website for current rates).

Your pay must be at least this amount unless a Modern Award specifies a higher rate.

Check current rate at: www.fairwork.gov.au/pay-and-wages/minimum-wages

3. Modern Awards

Many industries are covered by Modern Awards, which set minimum pay rates and conditions for specific industries or occupations.

Check if your role falls under an award at: www.fairwork.gov.au/awards

Common awards for administrative roles:

  • Clerks - Private Sector Award
  • Health Professionals and Support Services Award
  • Manufacturing and Associated Industries Award (if in manufacturing admin)

If you're covered by an award:

  • You must be paid at least the award rate for your classification
  • Being paid below award rate is wage theft
  • You may be misclassified at a lower level than your actual duties

4. Enterprise Agreements

Some workplaces have Enterprise Agreements that set pay and conditions. These override Modern Awards if they provide better conditions.

Check if your workplace has an Enterprise Agreement at: www.fwc.gov.au/agreements-awards/enterprise-agreements

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOUR NEGOTIATION:

✅ If you're being paid less than men doing comparable work, that's potentially a breach of equal pay provisions

✅ If you're being paid below the Modern Award rate for your actual role, that's wage theft

✅ If you're classified incorrectly (e.g., called "Admin Officer Level 2" but doing "Admin Officer Level 4" work), you may be entitled to the higher classification rate

✅ If you're not getting proper pay increases or are being disadvantaged because of your gender, that may be discrimination

When Underpayment Becomes Discrimination

Not all underpayment is discrimination, but some is. Here's how to tell:

UNDERPAYMENT THAT'S NOT DISCRIMINATION (but still unfair):

  • Company pays everyone below market rate (systemic underpayment, but equal)
  • You negotiated badly when you were hired and accepted a low offer
  • You haven't asked for a raise and they haven't offered one
  • Company is struggling financially and has frozen all salaries equally

These situations are unfair, but they're not illegal discrimination.

UNDERPAYMENT THAT IS DISCRIMINATION:

✅ Gender-based pay gap

You're paid less than male colleagues doing the same or comparable work, with similar experience and performance.

Example: You're an Operations Manager earning $65K. A male Operations Manager with similar experience and responsibilities earns $85K.

✅ Age discrimination

You're paid less because of your age (either "too young" or "too old").

Example: "We can't pay you that much because you're close to retirement" or "You're too young for that salary level."

✅ Pregnancy/family discrimination

You were denied raises or opportunities due to pregnancy, parental leave, or family responsibilities.

Example: Male colleagues got raises while you were on parental leave, or you were told "we can't promote you because you might have more children."

✅ Sexual harassment retaliation

You reported harassment and subsequently were denied raises or opportunities.

Example: After filing a sexual harassment complaint, your performance review suddenly became negative and you were denied your usual annual increase.

✅ "Boys club" culture

Promotions and raises consistently go to men while qualified women are overlooked.

Example: Every operations manager role has been filled by a man despite multiple qualified women applying, and male managers consistently earn more.

HOW TO IDENTIFY IF YOUR UNDERPAYMENT IS DISCRIMINATION:

Ask yourself honestly:

Gender-based indicators:

  • Are there male colleagues with similar experience and responsibilities who earn more?
  • Have I been performing well but consistently denied advancement while men are promoted?
  • Do men with less experience or qualifications earn more than me?
  • Is there a pattern of women being paid less than men across the organization?

Family status indicators:

  • Was I treated differently after taking parental leave?
  • Have I been told (directly or indirectly) that my family responsibilities affect my value?
  • Did my career progression stall after having children while male colleagues continued advancing?

Retaliation indicators:

  • Did my treatment change after I complained about harassment or unfair treatment?
  • Were pay/promotion decisions reversed after I raised concerns?
  • Am I being excluded or marginalized after speaking up?

Pattern indicators:

  • Have I been told I'm "too aggressive" when men behaving similarly are called "assertive"?
  • Am I labeled "emotional" when raising valid business concerns?
  • Are men regularly given opportunities I'm denied despite comparable qualifications?

If you answered yes to multiple questions, you may have a discrimination case.

Your Rights in Salary Negotiation

As an Australian employee, you have these rights:

YOU HAVE THE RIGHT TO:

✅ Ask for a salary review without retaliation or adverse action

✅ Discuss your pay with colleagues

Your employer cannot prohibit you from discussing salaries. Policies that ban pay discussions may violate Fair Work Act provisions.

✅ Request information about pay structures

Though they may not have to provide detailed salary info about specific colleagues, you can request information about pay bands, classification levels, and general compensation structures.

✅ Refuse a lowball offer during hiring negotiations

You're not required to accept any offer, and declining an offer isn't grounds for adverse action if you're already employed.

✅ Bring a support person to meetings if you feel intimidated

You can request a support person be present in discussions about your employment.

✅ Make a complaint to Fair Work if you believe you're being discriminated against or experiencing adverse action

✅ Access your employment records

You have the right to request copies of your employment file, including performance reviews and job descriptions.

YOUR EMPLOYER CANNOT:

❌ Fire you for asking for a raise

This would constitute adverse action (taking action against you for exercising workplace rights).

❌ Retaliate against you for raising pay equity concerns

Retaliation for raising workplace concerns is prohibited under adverse action provisions.

❌ Prohibit you from discussing salaries with colleagues

Pay secrecy policies that prevent employees from discussing compensation may be unenforceable.

❌ Pay you below Modern Award rates (if applicable)

If your role is covered by a Modern Award, paying below the award rate is wage theft.

❌ Discriminate against you based on gender, age, pregnancy, or family status

This violates anti-discrimination provisions in the Fair Work Act.

❌ Reduce your pay or conditions without agreement

Any change to your employment conditions that leaves you worse off requires your agreement or must follow specific legal processes.

ADVERSE ACTION PROTECTIONS

The Fair Work Act protects you from "adverse action" for exercising workplace rights.

Adverse action includes:

  • Firing you
  • Demoting you
  • Changing your duties to be less favorable
  • Treating you differently in a negative way
  • Not hiring you, not promoting you, or denying training opportunities
  • Threatening to do any of the above

You're protected from adverse action for:

  • Making a complaint or inquiry about your employment
  • Asserting your workplace rights
  • Refusing to do something that would be illegal
  • Participating in industrial action
  • Being a union member

If you believe you've experienced adverse action:

  • Document everything immediately
  • Contact Fair Work Ombudsman: 13 13 94
  • You have 21 days to lodge a general protections application with the Fair Work Commission
  • Consider consulting an employment lawyer

HR vs. Direct Manager Approach

Should you go to HR or your direct manager first?

START WITH YOUR DIRECT MANAGER IF:

  • You have a reasonably good relationship with them
  • They've been supportive in the past
  • They have authority to approve raises (or can advocate effectively)
  • You believe they'll advocate for you
  • The issue is straightforward (market rate adjustment, role expansion)
  • There's no harassment or discrimination involved

Why start here:

  • Respects chain of command
  • Maintains relationship with manager
  • Allows manager to be your advocate
  • Faster resolution if they have authority

GO TO HR OR SKIP YOUR MANAGER IF:

  • Your manager is the problem (they've harassed you, discriminated against you, or are blocking your advancement unfairly)
  • You believe there's systemic discrimination (pattern across the organization)
  • You've already tried with your manager and were refused without valid reason
  • You're preparing a formal complaint
  • You want documentation of your concerns for legal purposes
  • Your manager has retaliated against you in the past
  • You believe there's wage theft (being paid below award rate)

Why go to HR:

  • They handle formal complaints
  • They have responsibility for compliance
  • They can investigate patterns of discrimination
  • They can document issues formally

GOING TO HR: WHAT TO KNOW

IMPORTANT: HR is not your friend. HR's job is to protect the company, not you.

That said, HR can be useful if:

  • You're documenting discrimination
  • You need formal policy information
  • You're escalating after your manager refused
  • You believe there's a legal risk to the company
  • You're building a case for potential legal action

What to bring to HR:

  • All your documentation (market research, evidence, refusal documentation)
  • Specific examples of pay disparities
  • Clear statement of what you're requesting
  • Professional, unemotional tone
  • Dates, times, and details of relevant conversations

  • "I'm going to sue" (save that for a lawyer)

  • Gossip about colleagues or personal complaints
  • Threats or ultimatums
  • Anything you wouldn't want repeated to your manager

  • They'll document your complaint
  • They may investigate (or they may not)
  • They may interview your manager and others
  • They'll likely take weeks or months to respond

  • They may offer you something to prevent legal action

  • Follow up in writing with a summary of your conversation
  • Request written confirmation of any commitments made
  • Set a specific follow-up date
  • Continue documenting everything

  • Do I have a case?
  • What are my options?
  • What are the potential outcomes?
  • What are your fees? (Some work on contingency or offer free initial consultations)
  • What's the timeline for this type of case?
  • What's my likelihood of success?

How to find an employment lawyer:

  • Your state's Law Society (e.g., Law Society of WA: www.lawsocietywa.asn.au, Law Society of NSW: www.lawsociety.com.au, etc.)
  • Search for "employment lawyer Perth"
  • Ask for referrals from Legal Aid or community legal centers
  • Check reviews and specializations

Typical costs:

  • Initial consultation: Free to $300

  • Some work on contingency (percentage of settlement)
  • Some offer fixed-fee arrangements
